How Michigan property taxes work

Michigan homes have a taxable value used for property taxes. After Proposal A, that taxable value usually rises each year by the lesser of inflation or 5%, plus additions and minus losses. When a property transfers ownership, taxable value can reset closer to market value for the next tax year.

What this table isolates

This model assumes the home is not sold again and has no additions, losses, exemptions, or assessment drops. It then applies Michigan's capped-value inflation multipliers and Ann Arbor principal-residence millage rates to show the tax change from the purchase year baseline.

How to read the shares

The inflation column asks: what if only taxable value changed and the purchase-year millage stayed fixed? The millage column is the remaining effect of Ann Arbor's rate changing. Negative millage shares mean rates offset part of the inflation-driven increase.
